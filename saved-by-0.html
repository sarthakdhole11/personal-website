saved by zero (or why you should pick an aphorism)

the fixx, abai, post-punk, fisher  ·  18 january 2026  ·  almaty, kazakhstan

listening: “saved by zero” — the fixx

spotify, apple music, youtube

This is not intended to be a philosophical paper, although the question of whether it is one is philosophical in itself. It is an expanded view into an idea I have been engaging with for about a year now, across conversations in very different spheres of life.

Maybe someday, saved by zero, I’ll be more together; stretched by fewer thoughts that leave me chasing… The Fixx, Saved by Zero; Bauman, Liquid Modernity (2000)

Zygmunt Bauman’s assertion that freedom cannot be gained against society seems, at first glance, almost Machiavellian. However, Bauman is not offering advice to rulers but reflecting on the structure of the self within modern social conditions. He is diagnosing, not prescribing.

Liberation, in Bauman’s sense, does not mean the absence of constraint. It means the ability to act meaningfully within a world of shared expectations, institutions, and language. Outside of that framework, autonomy becomes performative rather than productive.

Rousseau’s social contract offers a useful parallel. In exchanging natural liberty for civil liberty, individuals accept constraint in order to gain stability, predictability, and mutual recognition. Absolute freedom in the state of nature does not translate into practical agency.

Kant sharpens this distinction further. For him, freedom is not acting on impulse but acting in accordance with laws one rationally endorses. Social norms can embody these laws, but only when they withstand ethical scrutiny. Submission without examination is not freedom.

Sartre would object here. Roles, norms, expectations — all are forms of bad faith. Yet radical autonomy without shared structures collapses into isolation. Choice requires context; language requires community; meaning requires continuity.

Adler’s emphasis on horizontal relationships points toward a social freedom grounded in cooperation rather than domination. One can belong without being absorbed, participate without being erased.

Bauman, then, is not arguing for obedience. He is arguing for embeddedness. Freedom is not found in flight from society, but in shaping it, contesting it, and negotiating one’s place within it.

In that sense, the aphorism matters. It is brief, repeatable, mobile. It survives in unstable contexts. It does not explain everything, but it holds long enough to orient thought.

Maybe that is what being saved by zero means — not escaping the system, but resetting within it, momentarily, before re-entering with fewer illusions of purity.
